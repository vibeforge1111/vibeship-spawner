# Patterns: Brand Positioning

These are the proven approaches that consistently create strong, defensible brand positions.

---

## 1. The Positioning Statement Framework

**What It Is:**
A structured way to articulate brand positioning in a single, focused statement.

**When To Use:**
- Defining or refining brand positioning
- Aligning teams on brand direction
- Briefing agencies and creative partners
- Evaluating brand decisions

**The Pattern:**

```
For [target customer]
Who [statement of need or opportunity]
[Brand name] is the [category]
That [key benefit/promise]
Because [reason to believe]
Unlike [competitive alternative]
Our brand [primary differentiation]

Example - Volvo:

For safety-conscious families
Who prioritize protecting their loved ones above all
Volvo is the automotive brand
That is built around protecting life
Because of our 60+ year obsession with safety innovation
Unlike luxury brands focused on status or performance
Our brand makes safety beautiful and aspirational
```

**Why It Works:**
Forces strategic clarity by answering all positioning questions in sequence. Creates a single source of truth for brand decisions. Makes positioning testable and debatable.

**Validation Questions:**
- Can you defend each element?
- Would competitors struggle to make the same claim?
- Does target customer recognize themselves?
- Is the category claim credible?

## 2. The Category Design Play

**What It Is:**
Creating a new category rather than competing in an existing one, positioning yourself as the category king.

**When To Use:**
- You can't be #1 or #2 in existing category
- Your product is genuinely different in kind, not just degree
- Market is ready for new mental model
- You have resources for category education

**The Pattern:**

```
Step 1: Identify the Category Opportunity
- What assumption do existing categories make that you reject?
- What's the "before and after" transformation you enable?
- What trend makes a new category timely?

Step 2: Name the Category
- Use a two-word formula: [Modifier] + [Known Category]
- Make it intuitive but novel
- Ensure it can scale (not too narrow)

Examples:
- "Cloud CRM" (Salesforce)
- "Conversational Marketing" (Drift)
- "Revenue Intelligence" (Gong)
- "Customer Data Platform" (Segment)

Step 3: Define the Category
- Create the category's "rules" (you wrote them)
- Define what's "in" and "out"
- Establish evaluation criteria (you win on your criteria)

Step 4: Evangelize the Category
- Talk about the category more than your product
- Help analysts understand the category
- Get others to participate (validates the category)
- Be the generous expert on the category
```

**Why It Works:**
Being first in a category is more valuable than being better in an existing one. Ries/Trout: "It's better to be first than to be better." The category king typically captures 76% of category economics.

## 3. The Enemy Positioning

**What It Is:**
Defining your brand by what you oppose, creating a clear "us vs. them" dynamic.

**When To Use:**
- Established players dominate current conversation
- Your audience feels underserved or frustrated
- You have a genuinely different philosophy
- You're comfortable being polarizing

**The Pattern:**

```
Structure:
"[Enemy] represents [old way/problem]. We represent [new way/solution]."

Examples:

Apple (1984):
- Enemy: IBM (Big Brother, conformity, corporate control)
- Us: Creative individuals who "think different"

Dollar Shave Club:
- Enemy: Overpriced razor blades from faceless corporations
- Us: Honest value, direct relationship, no bullshit

Basecamp:
- Enemy: Bloated, complex project management tools
- Us: Simple, calm, profitable business software

Steps:
1. Identify the dominant player or approach
2. Find legitimate criticism (not straw man)
3. Position yourself as the antidote
4. Make the contrast visceral and emotional
```

**Why It Works:**
Humans are tribal. Enemy positioning creates instant identity and community. It simplifies the buying decision ("I'm not like THEM"). It generates word-of-mouth (people love sharing opposition).

**Warning:**
Don't pick a fight you can't win. Don't be petty or mean-spirited. Attack the philosophy/approach, not the people.

## 4. The Transformation Positioning

**What It Is:**
Positioning based on who the customer becomes, not what the product does.

**When To Use:**
- Product enables personal/professional transformation
- Functional benefits are similar to competitors
- Audience has aspirational identity
- Emotional connection matters

**The Pattern:**

```
Formula:
"[Brand] transforms [current state] into [desired state]"

Examples:

Nike: Transforms regular people into athletes
- Not: "Shoes with air cushioning"
- But: "If you have a body, you're an athlete"

Peloton: Transforms home into a fitness studio
- Not: "Exercise bike with screen"
- But: "The best studio classes, at home"

Slack: Transforms chaotic teams into aligned teams
- Not: "Messaging app for work"
- But: "Where work happens" (implying: smoothly)

Structure:
1. Define customer's current frustrating state
2. Define their aspirational state
3. Position brand as the bridge
4. Prove you can deliver the transformation
```

**Why It Works:**
People don't buy products; they buy better versions of themselves. Transformation positioning taps into identity and aspiration, creating emotional connection beyond functional value.

## 5. The Ownership Positioning

**What It Is:**
Owning a single word or concept in the customer's mind.

**When To Use:**
- You can credibly claim a specific territory
- The word/concept matters to customers
- No competitor currently owns it
- You can consistently deliver on it

**The Pattern:**

```
Great Ownership Examples:

Volvo → Safety
BMW → Driving (performance)
Mercedes → Prestige
Toyota → Reliability
Tesla → Innovation/Future

FedEx → Overnight
Google → Search
Kleenex → Tissues (category)
Zoom → Video meetings

Process:
1. List words that matter in your category
2. Map which competitors own which words
3. Find unclaimed but valuable territory
4. Commit everything to owning that word
5. Prove ownership through consistent delivery

Ownership Test:
"When I think of [word], what brand comes to mind?"
If your brand doesn't immediately answer, you don't own it.
```

**Why It Works:**
The human mind craves simplicity. In an overcrowded market, the brand that owns one word has a shortcut directly into memory. Trying to own multiple words means owning none.

## 6. The Values-Led Positioning

**What It Is:**
Positioning based on shared values and beliefs with your target audience.

**When To Use:**
- Your audience makes value-based purchasing decisions
- You have authentic values worth championing
- Competitors are value-neutral or opposite
- You're willing to exclude customers who don't share values

**The Pattern:**

```
Structure:
"We believe [value]. That's why we [action]."

Examples:

Patagonia:
- Value: Environmental responsibility
- Action: "Don't buy this jacket" campaign, 1% for the Planet
- Position: The responsible outdoor brand

Ben & Jerry's:
- Value: Social justice
- Action: Activism, fair trade, climate advocacy
- Position: Ice cream with a conscience

TOMS:
- Value: Giving back
- Action: One-for-one model
- Position: Purchase with purpose

Process:
1. Identify authentic values (not manufactured)
2. Find values that differentiate (not universal "quality")
3. Take action that proves values (not just words)
4. Accept you'll alienate some customers
5. Build community around shared values
```

**Why It Works:**
64% of consumers choose brands based on shared values. Values create loyalty beyond product satisfaction. They make your brand worth defending and sharing.

## 7. The Heritage/Origin Positioning

**What It Is:**
Positioning based on where you come from, how you were founded, or your unique history.

**When To Use:**
- You have a compelling founding story
- Origin conveys quality, authenticity, or values
- Competitors lack distinctive heritage
- Story resonates with target audience

**The Pattern:**

```
Heritage Types:

Geographic:
- "Swiss watches" (precision)
- "German engineering" (quality)
- "California wine" (lifestyle)
- "Italian leather" (craftsmanship)

Founder Story:
- Started in a garage (authenticity)
- Founded by experts (credibility)
- Born from frustration (empathy)
- Immigrant founder (determination)

Method/Process:
- Handmade
- Small batch
- Traditional methods
- Scientific approach

Examples:
Jack Daniel's → Tennessee tradition, unchanged recipe
Levi's → Invented jeans for gold rush workers
Burt's Bees → Started by a beekeeper in Maine
```

**Why It Works:**
Heritage provides authenticity that can't be copied. Origin stories humanize brands. Provenance implies quality and care.

**Warning:**
Heritage must be authentic and verifiable. Fabricated heritage backfires spectacularly.

## 8. The Niche Dominance Strategy

**What It Is:**
Positioning as the definitive leader for a specific, narrow segment.

**When To Use:**
- You can't out-resource large competitors
- A valuable niche is underserved
- Deep expertise matters
- Niche can expand over time

**The Pattern:**

```
Formula:
"The [category] for [specific segment]"

Examples:
- Shopify: E-commerce for small merchants
- Mailchimp: Email marketing for small business
- Webflow: Website builder for designers
- Linear: Issue tracking for product teams

Niche Selection Criteria:
1. Underserved by generalists
2. Willing to pay for specialized solution
3. Clear identity/community
4. Adjacent niches for expansion
5. Reachable through targeted channels

Execution:
1. Deeply understand niche needs
2. Build features generalists won't
3. Speak niche language
4. Be present in niche communities
5. Get known by niche influencers
```

**Why It Works:**
It's easier to dominate a niche than compete broadly. Niche leadership creates word-of-mouth within tight communities. You can expand from a position of strength.

## 9. The Price-Tier Positioning

**What It Is:**
Explicitly positioning at a price tier: premium, value, or ultra-luxury.

**When To Use:**
- Price is a key buying factor
- You can deliver meaningfully at your tier
- Tier aligns with target audience
- You'll consistently execute at tier level

**The Pattern:**

```
Premium:
- Position: "Worth the premium because..."
- Execution: Higher quality, better service, exclusivity
- Examples: Apple, Mercedes, Whole Foods
- Trap: Premium without premium experience

Value:
- Position: "All you need, none of the bloat"
- Execution: Stripped down, efficient, honest
- Examples: Southwest, IKEA, Costco
- Trap: "Cheap" vs. "value" perception

Ultra-Luxury:
- Position: "The finest, for those who appreciate finest"
- Execution: Scarcity, craftsmanship, service
- Examples: Hermès, Rolls Royce, Patek Philippe
- Trap: Discounting or overexpansion

Middle (Danger Zone):
- No clear position
- "Good quality at fair prices"
- Stuck between premium and value
- Examples: Many department stores, commodity tech
```

**Why It Works:**
Price tier creates instant mental category. Customers self-select based on tier affinity. Consistent tier execution builds expectations and loyalty.

## 10. The "Only" Positioning Test

**What It Is:**
A simple test to validate positioning uniqueness and credibility.

**When To Use:**
- Evaluating potential positioning
- Stress-testing current positioning
- Comparing positioning options
- Simplifying complex positioning

**The Pattern:**

```
Complete this sentence honestly:

"We are the only [category] that [differentiation]."

Rules:
- Must be literally true
- Must be meaningful to customers
- Must be sustainable (not easily copied)
- Must be provable

Examples:

Strong:
- "We are the only cloud CRM built for sales teams" (early Salesforce)
- "We are the only delivery service that guarantees overnight" (early FedEx)
- "We are the only beauty brand founded on science" (early Clinique)

Weak:
- "We are the only CRM that cares about customers" (unverifiable)
- "We are the only fast food with quality" (subjective)
- "We are the only tech company that's innovative" (generic)

If you can't complete the sentence, you don't have positioning.
```

**Why It Works:**
Forces brutal honesty about differentiation. "Only" can't be faked - either it's true or it isn't. If competitors can make the same claim, it's not positioning.
